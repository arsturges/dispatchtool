About the Demand Response Dispatch Tool

Demand Response (DR) resources play an important role in transmission and capacity expansion modeling and in integrated resource planning. DR resources have the ability to reduce system peak load and/or shift load from system peak to other times in the day. This peak reduction and peak shifting may reduce the need to build new generation and transmission, reducing costs and providing environmental benefits.

The Demand Response Dispatch Tool (DRDT) was developed by Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL) to create load modifying profiles of Demand Response (DR) resources for use by regional transmission planners in the Western Interconnect. The DRDT uses DR resource availability and price information to develop a realistic dispatch approach for economic DR resources. The DRDT was initially created to develop inputs to PROMOD, a production cost model, but the load modifying profiles can be used more widely by other production cost and planning models.

How does The Demand Response Dispatch Tool work?

The Demand Response Dispatch Tool (DRDT) works by dispatching demand response resources during high-price hours or high-load hours according to resource availability. At a high level, the DRDT first characterizing a resource in terms of its program attributes, and then utilizes that resource in a resource-planning context with respect to its characteristics.

The availability of DR programs varies by month and by hour, for some programs. It is important to capture this availability in the modeling of DR resources, in order to ensure DR programs typically used during system peak months and hours are not utilized at full availability in non-system-peak months and hours.

What is a dispatchable demand response resource?

A demand response resource refers to any reductiong in electric load. The DRDT models dispatchable response programs which are capable of reducting load in response to dispatch signals. These signals come from the grid operator in real-time. The demand response resources used in the DRDT are based on NERC-definitions of dispatchable demand response resources.

The user provides the maximum monthly demand response resource size. For example, in August 2012 the maximum demand response resource might by 100 megawatts. Internally, the DRDT uses calculates based on the annual peak load how much of that total potential demand response resource is actually available to reduce load. For example, if the annual peak load was 4000 megawatts and the current load is 2000 megawatts, then only 50% of the 100 megawatts of the demand response resource is actually available to reduce load. The other 50% is not active, and so can't be reduced.
